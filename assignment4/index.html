Final Reflection

Overall, the entire process was quite demanding. To produce captivating visuals, I must understand the dataset front and back. If the dataset aids the argument without much manipulation, then we are blessed since this is probably not the case usually. However, it is still crucial to understand how to manipulate the data by grouping, binning, or calculating new fields. The manipulations allow us to accentuate particular aspects to further our argument. The same idea applies to datasets that do not support our argument well. I learned to use different means to obscure the flaws and highlight the little bit of "goodness" the dataset has. For example, instead of focusing solely on the actual numbers of the data, I can treat the numbers as binary (-1 or 1) to see if it can help. This method allows me to create persuasive visuals to support my proposition even though the dataset does not support it.

After this experience, I can say that any analysis and visualization is ethical as long as they don't directly manipulate the raw data and filter out significant chunks of the dataset. It's also tough to define what's considered ethical. When you are trying to argue for a position, who can blame you if you use every means possible to further your argument? If the deceptions are not obvious, then it is considered clever. If we can effortlessly point out the deceptions at a glance, then it is considered misleading. As for acceptable, if the visuals can deceive me, then I can accept anything. It's hard to draw a line since there are so many different aspects to consider depending on the topics.
